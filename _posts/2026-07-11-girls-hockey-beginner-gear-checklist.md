If your daughter just made a team, or just decided she wants to try, the equipment list can feel like it was written to overwhelm you on purpose. It wasn't — hockey genuinely needs more gear than almost any other youth sport. But you don't need to buy it all at once, and you definitely don't need the most expensive version of everything.

Here's the checklist I hand to every hockey parent I coach, in the order I'd actually buy it.

## Buy first: the safety gear

This is non-negotiable and where fit matters most. A helmet or shin guard that's slightly wrong will get worn incorrectly, which defeats the point.

## Buy second: skates and stick

## Buy third: the extras that make practice bearable

- **Hockey socks** (the tube-style ones that go over shin guards) — $15–25
- **Practice jersey** — often provided by the team; check before buying
- **Skate laces (spare pair)** — always keep a backup in the bag
- **Water bottle with a squeeze top** — easier to drink from with a mouthguard in
- **A separate bag** for wet gear after practice, so it doesn't mildew the rest of the equipment bag

## What it actually costs, all in

Budget **$300–450** for a genuinely no-frills first full setup (safety gear + skates + stick, bought secondhand where sensible), or **$600–900** buying everything new at mid-range prices. Either way, the single best money-saving move is buying a stick and pads that fit *now*, not gear she'll "grow into" — oversized protective gear doesn't protect properly and often gets abandoned because it's uncomfortable.

## One thing no one tells new hockey parents

The gear list looks like the hard part. It isn't. The hard part is the first few weeks of learning to skate with 15 pounds of extra weight on, before any of it starts to feel normal. Every player on the team you're worried about outfitting correctly went through that exact same awkward stretch. It gets easier fast — usually faster than the parents expect.
